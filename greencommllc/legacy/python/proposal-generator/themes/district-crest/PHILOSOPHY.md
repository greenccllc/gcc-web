# District Crest
### A visual philosophy for the GCC LLC proposal system — K-12 / public-institution audience.

---

## The movement

**District Crest** is designed for the reader on the other side of a school-board packet — the superintendent, the CFO, the facilities director, the principal who signs the line. It borrows the visual grammar of institutional stationery: a crest-inspired header band, **deep civic blue** with a small **gold** keyline, a serif that feels like a public-record letterhead, and a plainspoken proposal body that respects the reader's time. The document looks like it already belongs in the district's board binder, because that is where it will live.

## Surface and proportion

The palette is **district blue** (a deep navy with a hint of slate), **board gold** (muted brass, not metallic), **warm white** paper, and **graphite** for text. A slim blue band frames the top of the first page, closed on the right with a small gold square — the visual echo of a district seal. Body copy is set in a readable serif (Libre Baskerville) with generous leading. Tables are clear and generously spaced, formatted like a board-agenda appendix. Small gold brackets and blue kickers structure the page without ever raising voice.

## Tone and hierarchy

Headings are direct — *Scope. Schedule. Warranty. Acceptance.* — in a tone consistent with how district administrators speak to one another. Numbers are set in a serif mono (IBM Plex Mono) so they carry the weight of a public record. Summer-window schedule and prevailing-wage compliance are surfaced at the top of the page, because they are the first questions every district reader will ask. Every element is tuned with painstaking attention to the conventions of public-work documentation — the product of someone who has read a lot of board packets and remembers what they were missing.

## The temperament

District Crest is the proposal as *packet-ready attachment*. It behaves well in a binder, scans cleanly on a black-and-white copier, and reads as respectful of the institution's process and people. Master-level craftsmanship shows in the civic restraint: the document never tries to be more interesting than the district's agenda itself, and that is exactly why it earns the signature.
