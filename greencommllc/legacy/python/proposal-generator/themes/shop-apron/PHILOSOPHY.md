# Shop Apron
### A visual philosophy for the GCC LLC proposal system — warm, plainspoken, quietly funny.

---

## The movement

**Shop Apron** is the proposal with its sleeves rolled up. Not cartoony, not goofy — but warm, plainspoken, written by a human who actually pulls cable for a living and is not above a dry aside. The palette is **canvas tan** (the tan of a real apron), **ink black**, **safety orange** as a single punctuating accent, and a warm **masking-tape yellow** for the kicker tags. Headings speak in the voice of a foreman: *The Work. The Number. The Handshake.* The document is confident enough to be casual without ever becoming sloppy.

## Surface and voice

The ground is a warm canvas tan. Headlines set in a condensed sans (Big Shoulders or Work Sans Bold), tight and honest — the typographic equivalent of a label written in marker on a tool chest. Body copy is set in a humanist serif at a comfortable reading size. A single safety-orange rule runs the length of the scope section. Section kickers are set on small masking-tape-yellow rectangles, like a piece of labeled tape on a conduit run. Numbers are set in Geist Mono — a modern, warm mono that reads friendly rather than clinical.

## Tone and rhythm

Headings are *short sentences*, not titles: "Here is the work." "Here is what it costs." "Here is when we finish." The footer carries a small dry kicker — *"No surprises. No hidden truck charges. If the price changes, we say so first."* Numbers are bolded at the total line, because that is the one place the proposal does not want to be cute. The composition is calm — masking-tape kicker tags and the orange spine are the only playful notes, and they are used sparingly, like a good joke: one per page, perfectly timed, never twice.

## The temperament

Shop Apron is the proposal for a client who wants a human — a residential homeowner, a small-business owner, a repeat GC who appreciates not being spoken at in consultant-ese. It is professional, but it reads like the person who wrote it could also rewire a panel. Master-level craftsmanship shows in the restraint: the humor is not in the words, it is in the *tone*, and the tone is the work of someone who has learned that the funniest people in any room are also the most prepared.
