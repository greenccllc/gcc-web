# Bone Editorial
### A visual philosophy for the GCC LLC proposal system — warm beige option.

---

## The movement

**Bone Editorial** is the proposal reimagined as a long-form magazine spread. Think of a feature in *Monocle* or *The World of Interiors*: warm beige and bone stock, a single large serif headline set in restrained proportion, body copy that reads like an essay rather than a data dump. The palette is **bone**, **warm taupe**, **soft camel**, and a single accent of **ember orange** — the color of late-afternoon sun on a plastered wall. The work is text-forward and typographically generous, with numbers embedded in prose-like rows instead of filling aggressive tables.

## Surface and tone

The ground is a warm **bone** cream, the kind of stock a fine-press publisher would specify. Accents appear as sparing tonal shifts: a camel underline beneath a column total, an ember-orange drop cap starting a section, a small taupe pilcrow to mark a footnote. Numbers are set in a warm serif (IBM Plex Serif) so they sit beside the body prose rather than fighting it. Nothing blocks. Nothing shouts. The page breathes the way a well-edited magazine spread breathes.

## Voice and proportion

Headings are set in a generous display serif — Gloock or Crimson — at large sizes, with extra leading. Body copy uses Lora at a relaxed 10.5 pt with open line-height. Sections flow as chapters, not modules. A small keyline or column of tracked-out kickers anchors each section. Every element is placed with the calm precision of an editorial designer who has laid out countless features and reached the level where the decisions look inevitable.

## The temperament

Bone Editorial is the proposal as *essay* — the work of a firm that respects the client's time enough to write rather than merely list. It suits architect-led projects, hospitality renovations, private clients, and any audience who values taste as evidence of rigor. Master-level craftsmanship reveals itself in what is *not* on the page: no logos scattered, no bolded totals, no double-underscores. Restraint is the argument.
